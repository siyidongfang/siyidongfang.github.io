Antarctic Undercurrents and Nonlinear Ocean-Sea Ice-Ice Shelf Interactions (in prep)

Near the Antarctic margins, wind stress and tides, the primary forces of the ice-ocean system, inject westward momentum into the slope current. Counterintuitively, both observations and modeling studies have confirmed the existence of eastward undercurrents, which are opposite to the wind direction, along the continental shelf break of West Antarctica. These undercurrents are critical to the melting of the west Antarctic ice shelves because when encountering submarine troughs, they transport warm Circumpolar Deep Water directly towards ice shelf cavities. However, it remains unclear what drives the undercurrents, and how sensitive the ice shelf melt rate is to undercurrent change.

In this study, the dynamics of the West Antarctic undercurrents are investigated using a high-resolution process model with coupled ocean, sea ice and ice shelf. Over a wide range of parameters, we explore the sensitivities of the undercurrent strength, shoreward heat transport, and ice shelf melt rates to winds, tides, surface buoyancy forcing, diapycnal mixing, and geometry in a steady state. In addition, the transient responses to seasonal/interannual cycles and abrupt changes in the forcings are investigated. The formation mechanisms of the undercurrents are revealed by the momentum and heat budgets, as well as potential-kinetic energy conversions.

It is found that the undercurrent is forced by the cross-slope buoyancy gradient, and thus is strongly modulated by processes that change the isopycnal geometry, such as buoyancy forcing and diapycnal mixing. This study advances our understanding of the nonlinear interactions between ocean, sea ice, and ice shelves. Moreover, our results have implications for future studies on the stability and melt rate of the west Antarctic ice shelves using models with increased complexity.
