“Hot spots” of Ocean Heat Transport toward Antarctic Glaciers (in prep)

Transfer of oceanic heat toward the Antarctic margins is important to Earth's climate, as it influences melting of ice shelves, formation of bottom water, global meridional overturning circulation, and sea-level rise. However, the environmental conditions around Antarctica create difficulties for measurements, and therefore limit the dynamical insight available directly from observations. This issue can be overcome through numerical modeling guided by available observations.

Though previous circum-Antarctic simulations have revealed many dynamics of the Antarctic margins, they have significant caveats, e.g., they did not include ice shelves and/or did not fully resolve eddies over the shelf and slope due to the coarse resolution. It remains unclear where “hot spots” of ocean heat transport toward Antarctic glaciers occur, and to what extent small-scale and/or high-frequency variability such as eddies, tides, and dense outflows modulate onshore heat transport.

This study aims to close the scientific gaps by developing a high-resolution Southern Ocean regional model with coupled ocean, sea ice, and ice shelves. Various tools are developed to analyze onshore heat transport and ice-shelf melt rates in different sectors of the Antarctic margins, and advance the understanding of the underlying mechanisms. The roles of baroclinic eddies and dense outflows in heat transport and melt rates are investigated by temporal/spatial decomposition. Moreover, how ice dynamics and thermodynamics affect the along-slope circulation and cross-slope exchanges are also discussed.

The findings of this study have significant implications for sea-level rise and future climate change. In addition, our results help guide future climate model development, eddy heat flux parameterization, as well as future observations of near-Antarctic ocean heat flux and glacier melt.
