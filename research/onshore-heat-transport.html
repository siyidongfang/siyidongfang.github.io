Heat transport across the Antarctic Slope Front

Heat transport across the Antarctic Slope Front (ASF) is important to Earth’s climate, as it influences melting of ice shelves, the formation of bottom water, and thus the global meridional overturning circulation.

Previous studies based on relatively low-resolution models have reported contradictory findings regarding the impact of additional meltwater on onshore heat transport onto the Antarctic continental shelf: it remains unclear whether meltwater enhances shoreward heat transport, leading to a positive feedback, or further isolates the continental shelf from the open ocean.

We find that the shoreward heat transport is primarily controlled by the salinity gradient of the shelf waters: both freshening of the fresh shelf and salinification of the dense shelf lead to increased heat flux onto the continental shelf.

This implies a positive feedback in a warming climate: large volumes of meltwater increase shoreward heat transport, causing further melt of ice shelves.
