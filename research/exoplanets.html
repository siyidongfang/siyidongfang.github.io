Tide, ocean, and climate on exoplanets

For habitable planets around low-mass stars, the tidal force is expected to be much stronger than that on Earth, due to the fact that the habitable zone is very close to the host stars and that tide force is inversely proportional to the orbital distance cubed. On such planets, the tide-induced vertical oceanic mixing and therefore vertical diffusivity would be much greater than that on Earth.


In this study, we explore the effects of extremely high vertical diffusivity on the ocean circulation and surface climate of Earth-like planets. We find that a larger vertical diffusivity intensifies the meridional overturning circulation (MOC) in the ocean, which transports more heat to polar regions and melts sea ice there. Feedback associated with sea ice, clouds, and water vapor act to further amplify surface warming.


This study quantifies the climatic effect of an extremely strong vertical diffusivity and confirms an indirect link between planetary orbit, tidal mixing, ocean circulation, and surface climate.
